Fanuzzi version 1998-2004) I like the weird internet, the personal and idiosycratic internet, that can remind us that this box we’re sitting in front of or holding in our hand is capable of more than just tweets and horror politics and mediated entertainment. The internet that shapes its own little world. Not just “content” but also form. This summer, I went to an art/tech/culture festival called XOXO, and met a lot of wonderful people that are doing cool things at that intersection. One of my favorite events was the IndieWeb (https://indieweb.org/) meetup, a group of people that all share in some way this vision of the web as a place where you can create your own little unmediated universes (tilde club is an interesting throwback, for example). From there I found Mastadon (https://joinmastodon.org/), a decentralized social network that aims to be more humane, but comes with a learning curve I haven’t yet felt compelled to surmount. As neat as it is, my issue with Twitter etc. is less the ownership structure as much as the mediation. Email offers in some ways a portal back to the 90s internet, and has a directness and unmediated nature that is really cool. If you’re like me, most of the newsletters you get are probably marketing bullshit. Lately however, I’ve seen more and more friends, bands and public people start their own interesting newsletters, through free tools like MailChimp (which I use) and TinyLetter. This is encouraging. Hope you find this interesting, and thanks for reading. 2018: a year in review, so far I started lazerbea.ms almost a year ago to put out the work I was doing because: 1. I sincerely believe it to be important and worth sharing 2. I did not feel like convincing another label of this 3. I produce lots of work under many different identities and genres 4. thought I might formulate a unique approach to what a label is in our modern age, in aesthetic and philosophical cohesion. This so far has yielded 7 LPs, 1 EP, 2 music videos, 1 live performance (and live video), a manifesto, style guide, cards, a system of music analysis and visualization, and now an essay exploring the underpinnings of generative art. On the one hand, this is wonderful. On the other, this means that when I attempt to begin to explain what/why/how lazerbea.ms is, I don’t know where to begin, but Lazerbeams: Tonite! seems like the best place, because all of this began by chance: LB:2N is a duo project of myself and Axton Frick that began back in 2016. We met many years prior to that while we were both still living in New York, when we’d played together in a rock band called Krinkle Bearcat. The musical/personal connection was obvious and fast, but alas, as people sometimes do, he moved to Australia and we’d lost touch. Fast forward to spring of 2015, when after a lifetime in New York, I was finally going to be moving to Portland, a place my partner and I had wanted to live for ages. On our final day of our final visit before the move, we ambled down the PSU park blocks, where a market is held. On my left, a man slinging steaks caught my eye. I could not believe it. Wilford Brimley! No, no, it was Axton, and I was still crazy after all these years. After the move, I immediately started work on the studio that would become the Caged Unicorn. I have been an avid home recordist since 2002, but this was my first time beginning truly from scratch, and every aspect became intentional. As a multi-instrumentalist, I wanted to create a space where every instrument could always be ready to go, could be easily looped, sampled and modified, and most importantly, was intuitive. I created new interfaces and software with this in mind, for composing on the fly and making the first recording the finished one. A foundational principle of lazerbea.ms is to bring intention, the moments of creation, faults and all, to the fore. I want to immerse the listener. My first experiments with this lead to what became “we’ve got fucking atomic bombs”, an intense improvisation in some familiar forms. Shortly thereafter, Axton and I began recording together once more, in what was to become Lazerbeams: Tonite! Dear reader, I have always clung to familiar forms in my art. Despite my fondness for, say, Ornette Coleman or The Bad Plus or Autechre, these were not modes I felt comfortable operating in. Lazerbeams: Tonite! changed that. Axton and I had no idea what type of music we were going to create when we started, and together we were able to bring out something neither of us had felt free enough to do before. In a period of a few months we recorded a dozen sessions that jump between genres, not in a breakneck way like a John Zorn album, but in a fluid way that shows how the expressionistic impulse is what matters, and ultimately we are serving something outside ourselves.
